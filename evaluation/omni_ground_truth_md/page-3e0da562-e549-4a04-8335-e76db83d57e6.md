A point to be noted here is that, in the aforementioned disintegration of $ {\mu }_{x,y}^{\left( \mathrm{{ov}}\right) } $ , one only needs to look at the measures $ {\mu }_{t} $ for $ t \in \operatorname{Spec}\left( y\right) $ , and that every such $ {\mu }_{t} $ is supported on $ \operatorname{Spec}\left( x\right) $ , where $ \operatorname{Spec}\left( x\right) ,\operatorname{Spec}\left( y\right) \subseteq \mathbb{R} $ are the spectra of the elements $ x $ and $ y $ , respectively.

The name used for $ {\mu }_{x,y}^{\left( \mathrm{{ov}}\right) } $ in the present paper is *overlap measure* of $ x $ and $ y $ . Moreover, if $ {\mu }_{x,y}^{\left( \mathrm{{ov}}\right) } $ happens to be absolutely continuous with respect to the direct product $ {\mu }_{x} \times {\mu }_{y} $ of the distributions of $x$ and $y$, then we will refer to the Radon-Nikodym derivative of $ {\mu }_{x,y}^{\left( \mathrm{{ov}}\right) } $ with respect to $ {\mu }_{x} \times {\mu }_{y} $ by calling it the overlap function of $x$ and $y$ . We note that $ {\mu }_{x,y}^{\left( \mathrm{{ov}}\right) } $ does not always have to be absolutely continuous with respect to $ {\mu }_{x} \times {\mu }_{y} $ (consider for example $ y = x $ where $x$ has absolutely continuous distribution with respect to Lebesgue measure); but this will nevertheless be the case in several cases that are relevant for our calculations.

The motivation for the "overlap" terminology comes from the case when our $ \mathcal{A} $ is the algebra of matrices $ {\mathcal{M}}_{N}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) $ , considered with the normalized trace $ \varphi = \frac{1}{N}\operatorname{Tr} $, and when the pair of selfadjoint elements of $ \mathcal{A}$ to be considered is thus a pair $ \left( {{X}_{N},{Y}_{N}}\right) $ of complex Hermitian matrices. What happens here is that upon explicitly writing $ {X}_{N} $ and $ {Y}_{N} $ in terms of their eigenvalues and eigenvectors:

$$
$${X}_{N} = U\operatorname{diag}\left( {{\lambda }_{1},\ldots ,{\lambda }_{N}}\right) {U}^{ * }\text{ and }{Y}_{N} = V\operatorname{diag}\left( {{\rho }_{1},\ldots ,{\rho }_{N}}\right) {V}^{ * },$$
$$

with $ U = \left\lbrack {{\overrightarrow{u}}_{1},\ldots ,{\overrightarrow{u}}_{N}}\right\rbrack $ and $ V = \left\lbrack {{\overrightarrow{v}}_{1},\ldots ,{\overrightarrow{v}}_{N}}\right\rbrack $ , unitary matrices,

one easily finds that

$$
$${\mu }_{{X}_{N},{Y}_{N}}^{\left( \mathrm{{ov}}\right) } = \frac{1}{N}\mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i,j = 1}}^{N}{\left| \left\langle  {\overrightarrow{u}}_{i},{\overrightarrow{v}}_{j}\right\rangle  \right| }^{2}{\delta }_{\left( {\lambda }_{i},{\rho }_{j}\right) }.$$
$$

Moreover, the above measure is absolutely continuous with respect to the product of distributions (which in this case is just $ \frac{1}{{N}^{2}}\mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i,j = 1}}^{N}{\delta }_{\left( {\lambda }_{i},{\rho }_{j}\right) } $ , the direct product of the empirical eigenvalue distributions of $ {X}_{N} $ and $ {Y}_{N} $ ). The Radon-Nikodym derivative at a point $ \left( {{\lambda }_{i},{\rho }_{j}}\right) $ is given by $ N{\left| \left\langle {\overrightarrow{u}}_{i},{\overrightarrow{v}}_{j}\right\rangle \right| }^{2} $ - this is precisely the quantity called an overlap in the physics literature.
