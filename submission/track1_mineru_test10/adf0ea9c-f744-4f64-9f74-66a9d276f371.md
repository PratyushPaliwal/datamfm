## Part I

# Projection and introjection

## 1 The New Yorkers

## Introduction

Let us return to the New Yorker covers. We opened with the gridded flatland of America, which situated the identity of New Yorkers. It is a question of what New Yorkers are to themselves, and to others. These covers exemplified the psychoanalytic structures of projection and introjection. The subject organises its relations to the world according to inside and outside, by articulating its identity with respect to space. In the Introduction, we said the aim of this argument was to construct an account of architectural space that is an elaboration of the psychoanalytic account of the subject. This account is not about different mathematical spaces, or mechanical space (moving bodies), Newtonian or otherwise. Nor is it about the space of everyday experience (the 'what is it like for you'), an approach that leads to sampling and tasting. While experience has bearing on any subjective account of space, such phenomenologies begin their investigations as if space were a prior object of experience. While it seems difficult to see how one might begin otherwise, we contest both object and prior $^{1}$ . These are questions a subjective account of space cannot beg. We noted that the discovery of the unconscious posed a crisis for the architectural understanding of space. The present thesis about the space of the unconscious began with a joke about space. We said that A View of the World from 9th Avenue was funny because it revealed something about the subjective doubling of vision that could only be revealed within the brackets of a joke. The joke gives voice to an anxiety about something that can only be discharged with laughter and that is only discovered by the subject through subsequent interpretation. The diminutions of perspective reflected not just depth of field but also the self-importance of New Yorkers with respect to the world. These claims must now be unpacked in ways that amplify projection and introjection.

## A View of the World...

In the foreground: the blocky grid of buildings which are themselves blocky grids; the Louis Kahn traffic-analysis pattern. In the background: a thin Pacific rim. It is funny because New Yorkers see the world like that and they are bullish enough about New York to assume that everyone else does too. As if even the people clinging to China think the Lower West Side is the coolest place on Earth. Better still: as if they see themselves the way New Yorkers on Ninth Avenue see them. Inhabiting a strip. One could diagnose a third-person spatial dysfunction whose symptom was to always refer to someone else's there as your here. It would involve a predisposition to assume other people's views of your world as your own.

The cover is funny for another reason. New Yorkers do think that New York is the biggest and best, even if they have the smarts to laugh at it. They are laughing at themselves. This view of the world says as much about New Yorkers as it does about the people they are looking at. The view has an opposite sense that works through interpretation, not vision. An interpretation of why this picture is funny leads to a recognition by New Yorkers of their geopolitical foibles in the non-threatening form of a joke. The view is projected onto the world by the subject; the interpretation returns a picture of the subject to the subject via language.

Let us look at the first sense: what this cartoon says about perspective projection. A View of the World... is not the view of the world, and not because there is another view which is, with which it does not coincide, but because there is no objective view. The lie of this joke, that this view of the world is everyone's view, gives way to another truth, that all views are subjective. For the subject, every view is the view of someone. The view that comes back to the subject from the outside is just the view of an other. If the objectivity of the visual representation of space is undermined by this cover, it is not because it is confronted by a particularly persuasive image, a defeat of the objective by the rhetorical power of the subjective, but because it claims that every perspective is the view of a particular subject.

We formalise the relation of subject to image with the geometry of a triangle (Figure 1.1a). The subject, a New Yorker, occupies the projection point at the apex; the image becomes the baseline. The diagram applies to any image for the subject, at least any image whose relation to the subject could be described as a subject viewing an image. The image could be New Yorkistan as well as A View of the World..., or any other image, printed or in any other form. The diagram simply describes how a subject is positioned before the image, how its view of the image radiates outward, and stops at the image. The image is as opaque as paper. It equally describes the relation of the subject to an object. Lacan points out that this account of the relation of subject to the world accords with Descartes' cogito in Meditations, for which the world is an extension of the subject. $^{2}$

## New Yorkistan

The plan of Manhattan is drawn as if it were a map of New Yorkistan, one of those places like Afghanistan that Americans only find out about when they become the sites of natural or military disasters. The New York Times obliges by publishing a map of tribal demographics. The place has so few natural features, so little infrastructure that all the Times can muster is something tribal. It is not particularly attractive or exotic. It is the kind of 'foreign' that remains foreign because there is nothing really to know about the place. New Yorkistan is a plan of Manhattan from the point of view of someone for whom New York is a foreign land. It recalls the humour of A View of the World... (as if the Chinese saw themselves the way New Yorkers on Ninth Avenue see them) but in reverse: as if New Yorkers saw themselves the way people in Afghanistan see them: occupying a tribal demographic map. We can formalise this relation in the same manner as we did A View of the World... with the geometry of the triangle (Figure 1.2b). Except that now the projection point is on the side of the Other, and Manhattan is the screen upon which the image is projected. New Yorkers see an imaginary Manhattan projected from the position of that Pacific rim (the triangle is reversed so that New Yorkers occupy the same position as in Figure 1.2a). The world appears to gaze back. But if this seems to be a view of Manhattan from the outside, it is not exactly an Afghanistan's view, but a New Yorker's view of an Afghanistan's view (New Yorkistan is featured on a New Yorker cover, and not a Kabul daily). As if New Yorkers saw themselves the way they see people in Afghanistan. It is a New Yorker's view of Afghanistan directed back on him/herself. It is by this subjective circuit, in which New Yorkers see themselves through the eyes of others, or from the space they attribute to others with whom they cannot identify, that New Yorkers are brought to contemplate the possibility that they are strange to themselves.

## Introjection

This cartoon is funny because it turns out that the fear New Yorkers have of other places conceals an unacknowledged anxiety New Yorkers have about themselves. According to Freud, we project the bad object and introject the good one. If projection involves the subject expelling something from within and casting it upon the world, introjection involves the subject internalising features of the world into itself. The bad object returns to haunt us in the form of strange names, we experience it as anxiety, and we do our best to shield ourselves from it with jokes. $^{7}$

Although both processes involve crossing a psychic frontier between the subject's inner and outer worlds, it would be a mistake to think that introjection is simply the reverse sense of projection. For Lacan, contra Freud, introjection is of an entirely different order. Projection involves the image and the imaginary order (reverse projection is simply projection of an image by others). Introjection involves the internalisation of an object and relates to language and the symbolic order. It is therefore bound up with the discourse on objects that abounds in Lacan's text, which revolves around his archéobject objet $\alpha$ , and takes in the theory of desire and the drives.

We carve out the proper space of introjection with words and other signifiers. New Yorkistan is a plan, not a view, and in this plan, language takes pride of place. That the alienation provoked by this plan is an effect of language should be clear from

In Freud and Lacan, anxiety is associated with the imminent proximity of the unconscious object: the object that threatens to emerge from behind the image surface of consciousness. Underlying this is an anxiety about language itself, or at least language when it comes to us in the form of an image. Lacan gets close to this: if there is a model for strange words on a familiar surface, it is the writing on the walls in the Book of Daniel. This plan reminds us how strange naming is and how foreign is language when it intrudes upon our space. We do not name ourselves. Our name is chosen before we are born and pounded into us time and again by our mothers, along with other objects like breasts; Lacan calls her m(other) to underscore her status to the subject, preferred maybe but as other as any other other. The name may be a source of empowerment, but it is bound up with castration. With the name, the social order of language is imposed upon us and marks us forever. Virtually all our social relations go through language, everything from title deeds to jokes. Language feels as if it comes from inside us, but really it invades us. It is naturalised to the extent that we no longer retain a sense of its original strangeness. $^{9}$

## A topological view of manhattan

If A View of the World... reaffirms the familiar and crystalline spatial order of vision, the compensatory and opaque space of New Yorkistan opens to the spatial order of language. The effect of New Yorkistan derives from the fact that it is part of a transformation series of plans of Manhattan which begins with the street plan, and includes the boxy zip code plan published in the Yellow Pages, and the bloated plan upon which the subway system is traced. Its spatiality bears most allegiance to the hydraulic pushing and pulling of the subway map. Dark, noisy, often wet and always dirty. New Yorkistan has the whiff of internal organs about it, piled upon each other, collapsed unless full of air, food or shit. If you pull one out, then others would slip around a bit to fill up the vacancy. It is a wet squishy space like the inside of the human body ('introjection continues to bear the stamp of its bodily prototype [incorporation]'). $^{10}$

Plans use the letters of a name to represent a spatial distribution. The name not only nominates, it also shows where. 'Pashmina' (surrounding 'Shatoosh', south of 'Khlintunisia'), not only summons Pashmina, tests whether or not it exists; it also shows

us where Pashmina is in New Yorkistan, and - by the slightly radiating script set at an angle - intimates the expansive spatiality of the sweeping view (from Morningside Drive) and the flood plane (of Harlem below). There is a fleeting identity between 'Pashmina' and the area of Pashmina, the circumscribed surface upon which it is written. It is of the order of the I am here of graffiti - part word and part decoration and part gesture - which declares the position and physiognomy of a writer and his/her intersection with a wall. The spatiality of the name is felt most acutely in the momentary frisson when encountering someone else with the same name. Not only is the name now visibly from elsewhere, borne by an other, but it is as if for a moment you might not be where you are, or might violate the logic of space, that the same object cannot be in two places at the same time. Space seems to be other, and has no stability of location.

The name 'Pashima' and the surface on which it is written compete in the same space for reference to Pashmina, as if, in the matter of reference, the plan was over-determined. Although we can only access the space of the word through the graphic space of the plan, they are different. This linguistic space has to do with the network of relations that 'Pashmina' has to the other names in the plan, to the place names of Manhattan, and to the associations that New Yorkers bring to these made-up names ('Pashmina' = /≠ 'Pashtuns'). In this plan, signifiers shift inexplicably in relation to signifieds. This is one of the hallmarks of the foreign. For instance, Gaymenistan and the Lesbikhs are contiguous on the basis of two different types of association. The Gaymeni and Lesbikhs both hang out on the Lower West Side. Both groups are associated by their gender preferences. But on the other side of Central Pakistan, the Blahniks and the Bulimikhs are contiguous; this time the contiguity is based on alliteration. $^{11}$ In the former, the contiguity has to do with the signifieds, in the latter, with the signifiers. There is no apparent reason why it should be one way on the Hudson River and another on the East River. That's just the way it is with foreign lands and their customs. The successful tourist realises this, and comes back to his/her own culture enriched by the knowledge that it is equally conventional. $^{12}$

The adjacencies and separateness of, for example, 'Pashmina' or 'Blahniks' constitute a topological space that underlies but is not commensurate with the metric space of Pashmina or the Blahniks. Without a rigorous demonstration, we would like to claim for language the space of topology. Instead of the familiar properties (dimension, angle, shape, proportion) of architectural space in which every name is confined to its own room, topological space is organised into regions and their boundaries, in relations of inclusion, intersection and foreign-ness. 'Pashmina' is not 3m from 'Badassin'; it is separated from 'Badassin' by 'Khlintunisia'. We are familiar with the spatiality of words by the dream work which links latent to manifest content. (As Freud demonstrated in his walking talking tour of Signorelli/Herzegovina, names impress themselves with greater and lesser tenacity upon consciousness, and their strange allegiances come encumbered with sex and death.) $^{19}$ Words are separated by displacement and joined by condensation (what for Lacan is metonymy and metaphor). Words are not smaller because they are far away, they are only of greater or lesser use or familiarity, more or less tangled up with other words. New Yorkeristan is a battlescape between the competing spaces of the

image and the word. The word is written across the American landscape, whereas the metric precision of the plan represses the inherent spatiality of the name. $^{14}$

The term space is used in the most literal way. Not like a space, or as if a space, or space metaphorically speaking; except that if language is spatial, then perhaps everything is spatial in a metaphorical way. The social psychologist Kurt Levin takes the space of subjectivity literally (Figure 1.4). The question of whether a field of data constitutes a space depends upon whether there is a geometry that describes it, and allows for a definition of position. There are data fields in mathematics that constitute spaces because they have a geometry. The fact that a geometry does not correspond to the experiential space of vision - what Levin misleadingly calls empirical space - makes it no less spatial. $^{15}$

Topology is the simple space of containment. It is the most general science of spatial relations from which projective and Euclidean geometries are derived. For the subject, it corresponds to the fundamental experience and phenomenological aspect of space. We are first and foremost in space, in among our objects. We are included in it, surrounded by it. Even before we are in front of the image, or looking west on Ninth Avenue, or before the altar, we are in the city, or in the nave. When we stand before the image in which space is gathered around a vanishing point and we are positioned three bays back from the altar, we have already emerged from this more fundamental space. Whereas the image is visual, this space of inclusion may not necessarily be. It could be tactile, aural or olfactorial. It could be a surface or in three dimensions. Even the most formless space - white-out conditions, the open ocean (Freud's oceanic space) - retains its property of inclusion for the winter mountaineer or mariner, long after size, distance and direction have abandoned him/her. Topology is the geometry of shifter space. The answer to the question Where am 1?, in topological space is I am (in) here. This is the most basic form of spatial proposition, at least as concerns location. Location is given with respect to the shifter here that relates to the other shifter I. The shifter - I/you, here/there, this/that, now/then - is the sign of subjectivity. $^{16}$

Lacan was interested in topology because it enabled him to contemplate the forms of spatiality of the signifying chain and to escape Freud's reliance on overly simplistic spatial concepts to describe psychoanalytic structures. $^{17}$ According to Lacan, language has the structure of a Mobius strip, with signifiers on one side and signifieds on the other (the strip being the bar of signification), but which run together on its single surface (Figure 1.5a). The Mobius strip is a single surface with two sides. It is tempting to think you could drill an eyehole through it to see out the other side but you can also get there by travelling 360° around the strip without leaving or crossing its surface. A radical space slides beneath the plan of New Yorkistan, which is the foreign province of introjection, but no hole will get us to its other side.
