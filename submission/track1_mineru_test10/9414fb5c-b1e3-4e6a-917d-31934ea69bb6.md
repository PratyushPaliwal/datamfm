two-shift-per-day operation, well-designed processes and plants that run at a steady state enjoy considerable advantage with respect to human error. This stems less from process control and automation than from their operating rhythm and so is much less subject to forgetfulness, improvisations, and the lesser preparedness fostered by being able to put the next batch off to the next shift or fixing a mechanical problem when it might be convenient rather compelling. But again we digress; let us describe the continuous reactors useful in our context of thermochemical safety in pharma.

## 1. Tubular Reactors

Although design may vary a little, that basic principle is the same throughout--the geometrical advantage of a long and thin cylinder over a stirred tank that rarely operates at H/D >1.25, namely:

a. The relative ease in achieving turbulence and with it the very desirable uniformity of the dwell time (or residence time) of most of the process stream. If there is one attribute of the well-stirred batch mode that one might like is the fact that every volume element shares the same fate during the course of the reaction--if our kinetics model of the reactions is good, its predictions will be good. This attribute--a confidence builder if nothing else--can be pretty well approximated in tubular reactors in turbulent flow, which are not hard to design, build, and operate $[25]$ , chap. 11-16).  
b. The much higher surface area available for heat transfer per unit of reaction mass volume that is afforded by the spaghetti-like geometry of a long and thin tube, which makes it much easier to have a cooling capacity that exceeds the reaction heat release; that is, $Q_{\mathrm{ex}} > Q_{\mathrm{r}}$ .  
c. For fast and mixing sensitive reactions, the tubular reactor can bring together the reactants far better than the stirred tank batch and comparably to the best design in semibatch reactors (Fig. 34). If semibatch addition is best for reaction selectivity, multiple addition points are easily implemented in the tubular reactor.  
d. Finally, for reactions that need to be prompted before they go on their own (as in the case of Morton International), the tubular reactor design can incorporate a preheating section, followed by the cooling section for the exotherm and ending with a quenching section, in which the reaction can be brought to an abrupt slowing down (Fig. 35). These features--the ability to initiate in a quick heat-up, safely assimilate the exotherm, and then terminate just as quickly--are most useful when dealing with reactions that exhibit sharp ascending and descending yield curves as a function of conversion or reaction time at temperature, as in the racemization of the key methyldopa intermediate (26), which was successfully scaled up to ca. 400 mta per single pass reactor. A similar design has been documented for the 400 mta production of nicotinamide from 3-cyanopyridine (27) in a single tubular reactor.

Alas, the pernicious view of continuous reactors being only appropriate at large scale has probably sentenced a good deal of chemistry to less than optimal performance in batch and semibatch reactors. The success of a continuous reactor solving a problem of very fast, mixing sensitive and competitive reactions at a small pharma scale--<40 mta in a multiproduct plant running in

continuous process for the preparation and immediate use of methyl isocyanate. Instead, the processing was provided with a storage capacity of up to 45 tons of the compound in each of three storage tanks. Tragically, it appears that a process not requiring storage of methyl isocyanate was available (4).

## VII. THERMOCHEMICAL HAZARD AVOIDANCE, PREVENTION, AND RISK REDUCTION

## A. Hazards Upon Processing Abnormalities or Change

Several of the references at the end of this chapter include brief discussions of actual thermochemical accidents (1-4,15,16,17,18), and one is struck by how many of the cases could be attributed, in part at least, to processing abnormalities, many of which were mishandled, and to start-ups and changes in processing that were ill considered or poorly executed. Obviously, the role of a rigorous process change management system, with accountable ownership in the organization, is indispensable to safe thermochemical processing, and the reader is referred to chapter 3 for guidance and specifics on the subject. Also of note is the frequency of "Friday night" decisions that were troublesome over the weekend, which reinforces the extra alertness needed when the processing does not have a good operating rhythm or the good rhythm is interrupted.

## B. The Safe Process Envelope

The concept of the safe process envelope is simple: bring to bear all the process know-how and necessary design skills (not just those that are conveniently available) to define the boundaries of the process (as implemented at the plant scale) with a focus on thermochemical safety within the overall process safety effort. Initial boundaries need to be defined from the hazards assessment--the starting envelope (Fig. 19, chap. 2)--and then redefined as the development and process design interaction starts as early as possible (see Fig. 1, chap. 3) so that the implemented project is based on the contoured safe process envelope (see Fig. 21, chap. 3). Indeed, chapter 3 covers this ground extensively and deeply, and repetition herein is not warranted.

## C. The Role of Process Design Based on the Hazard Assessment

The meaning of the above heading can be stated briefly: the hazards assessment is considerably richer than the accumulation of test results, for it cannot be completed if the process design scenarios are not blended in with the risk assessment of the design solutions to the hazards being accepted in principle, some of which may have to be rejected upon assessing the risks. Once more, chapter 3 delves into the process design role with respect to assessing hazards and their risks for the implementation scenarios, and Figure 20 in that chapter depicts the working mechanics at hand.

## D. Formal Hazards Assessment of Process Operations

There are formal methods of operational hazards assessment (1,20,31,32,33), and it would be beyond the scope of this chapter to describe them--thus our deferring to the broader scope of chapter 11. We will just make the point that the technology transfer through process design and start-up of first manufacture must embrace the operational hazards and risk assessments.

## E. The Outsourcing Environment--Hazards and Risks

As the outsourcing business model of pharma manufacturing surges forward on a broad front (35), the appreciation of its hazards and risks must be foremost in the minds of those executing outsourced operations--both the client that needs the bulk drug and the supplier that needs the business. To this author, thermochemical process safety in the outsourcing mode of securing the supply of new bulk drugs is pregnant with hazards and risks (34), an issue that has been recognized by the American Institute of Chemical Engineers through the publication of a thoughtful "how to" document (34), which should be at the desk of everyone involved.

The situation is particularly hazardous when there is a significant transfer of process technology from the owner of the drug and its NDA to one or more contract suppliers, during which thermochemical process safety often does not get the attention it deserves and the relevant know-how disperses. Worst of all is the case of the virtual pharma organization that licenses a drug candidate with an incomplete process package and then needs the licensor--who has whatever process know-how exists--to go and help the supplier, who often has too high an opinion of its own skills and insists in putting its imprint on the process or shoehorning it into its plant. To his good fortune, the author had nothing but good experiences during his corporate career, due to the deep commitment of the client to the tech transfer and the good choices of conscientious and capable suppliers. Alas, that was long ago, and such have not been the author's observations in his postcorporate career, with thermochemical process safety often getting short shrift in many of the cases observed.

## VIII. IN CLOSING

The practice of thermochemical process safety--as one of the three pillars of chemical processing--is an activity based on skills, discipline, and focused attention at all levels and on both sides of every organizational boundary--the internal ones within a pharma organization and those between client and supplier. It is not a field to dabble in, for to practice in it there has to be a serious commitment of physical and human resources. And even those who choose to contract out hazard and risk assessment work need to have skilled people who can stay on top of the contracted work and see it applied properly wherever the implementation takes place.

## I. INTRODUCTION

Many hazardous chemicals are used in the production of active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs). These chemicals may be flammable/combustible liquids, toxic gases, water-reactive chemicals, and cyanides, to mention several types.

In addition, some final APIs may be health hazardous to operators working with them; for example, they may be carcinogenic, mutagenic, or teratogenic, and have to be contained to prevent them from coming in contact with the operators. These hazardous chemicals can result in fires, explosions, and releases of toxic gases or health-hazardous APIs if they are not controlled.

A number of technical organizations in the United States publish standards and guidelines for the safe design and operation of chemical and pharmaceutical plants. Among these organizations are American Institute of Chemical Engineers/Center for Chemical Process Safety (AIChE/CCPS) (www.aiche.org), American Petroleum Institute (API) (www.api.org), National Fire Protection Association (NFPA) (www.nfpa.org), FM Global (www.fmglobal.com), and International Society for Pharmaceutical Engineering (ISPE) (www.ispe.org).

In addition, all companies must comply with the regulations issued by the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) (www.osha.gov) and the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) (www.epa.gov).

In Europe, the International Standards Organization (ISO) (www.iso.org) and the Comité Européen de Normalisation (CEN) (www.cen.eu) publish standards and guidelines similar to those published by the U.S. companies mentioned earlier. Also, the U.K. Health and Safety Executive has published many documents on process safety and loss prevention (www.hse.gov.uk).

On July 1, 2003, the ATEX Directive became mandatory in Europe and governed by European law for all electrical and mechanical equipment used in potentially explosive atmospheres (as are often found in chemical and pharmaceutical plants). ATEX is the name commonly given to the framework for controlling explosive atmospheres in the workplace and the standards for equipment and protective systems used in them. More information on ATEX can be found on the U.K. Health and Safety Executive Web site.

This chapter discusses design and operating practices that can be used to eliminate or minimize the hazards mentioned previously so that the plant is operated in a safe manner, protecting the process equipment and operators.

## II. FLAMMABLE/COMBUSTIBLE LIQUIDS STORAGE AND HANDLING

## A. Storage

## 1. Introduction

Flammable and combustible liquids are stored in tanks in tank farms or in "day" tanks in buildings. Tanks in these farms are usually vertical atmospheric- or low-pressure tanks of the API type (fixed or floating roof) for large volumes, or

vertical or horizontal tanks of the ASME type for smaller volumes (usually up to about 50,000 gal). These ASME-type tanks are designed for about 14.5 psig so that they are not considered to be pressure vessels under section VIII of the ASME Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code, and do not require a code stamp or the use of an ASME-certified welder to make repairs. In pharmaceutical plants, "day" tanks are supported on legs so that the area under them can be easily washed down and cleaned.

NFPA 30 (1) defines a flammable liquid as one that has a closed-cup flash point below $100^{\circ}\mathrm{F}$ $(37.8^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ and a Reid vapor pressure that does not exceed an absolute pressure of 40 psi (276 kPa) at $100^{\circ}\mathrm{F}$ , and a combustible liquid as one that has a closed-cup flash point above $100^{\circ}\mathrm{F}$ . In addition, flammable liquids are classified as class IA, IB, and IC, as follows:

a. Class IA liquid--any liquid that has a flash point below $73^{\circ}$ F ( $22.8^{\circ}$ C) and a boiling point below $100^{\circ}$ F ( $37.8^{\circ}$ C)  
b. Class IB liquid--any liquid that has a flash point below $73^{\circ}$ F and a boiling point at or above $100^{\circ}$ F  
c. Class IC liquid--any liquid that has a flash point at or above $73^{\circ}$ F, but below $100^{\circ}$ F

Classifications for combustible liquids (class II and class III) are also defined in NFPA 30.

The liquid class affects such design considerations as location of tanks with respect to other tanks and equipment, property lines, and important plant buildings; normal and emergency venting; and requirements for conservation vents and/or flame arresters. Some of these design considerations and practices are discussed in the following sections.

Tank location and layout. Criteria for tank farm tank locations and installation are given in chapter 22 of NFPA 30. Design criteria are also presented for diking in chapter 22. For "day" tanks inside of buildings, distances between tanks and other equipment are usually based on the area needed for operator access and maintenance work.

Tank filling. Storage tanks in tank farms are quite often filled from tanker trucks or railcars. Before the transfer of liquid into the storage tank is begun, all equipment in the transfer system (truck or railcar, storage tank, and piping) must be properly bonded and grounded. Information on this can be found in NFPA 77 (2) and API RP 2003 (3). The bonding and grounding should be checked for electrical continuity before starting liquid transfer by using commercially available equipment for this.

Fill pipes that enter the top of a storage tank should terminate within 6 in. (150 mm) of the bottom of the tank. They should be installed or arranged so that vibration is minimized.

Day tanks inside buildings are usually filled from tanker trucks or drums. Bonding and grounding requirements and fill pipe design and installation should be the same as for tank farm tanks.

All storage tanks containing flammable or combustible liquids should be provided with overfill protection. The tank should be equipped with a high-level detection instrument (often a capacitance probe) that is independent of the

tank's normal level indication instrumentation, and is interlocked to close an open-shut valve in the fill line. The high-level detector can also be interlocked to shut down a pump that feeds liquid to the tank, and equipped with alarms that are located where personnel who are on duty throughout liquid transfer can arrange to shut down the pump or divert the flow to another item of equipment in accordance with established procedures. Figure 1 is a schematic of a typical overflow protection system.

Normal and emergency relief venting. Normal venting is provided to permit the intake and discharge of air during filling and emptying operations and to permit expansion and contraction of vapor due to temperature changes.

Criteria for normal venting of tanks and vent sizing are given in chapter 21 of NFPA 30. This chapter stipulates the following:

a. Provide normally closed venting devices, that is, conservation vents, on tanks storing class IA liquids.  
b. Provide normally closed venting devices, that is, conservation vents, or with an approved (listed) flame arrester, on tanks storing class IB or class IC liquids.

For manifolded vent systems, the following safety measures should be followed (4):

- Do not manifold vent collection systems of tanks containing incompatible chemicals.  
- Do not manifold vent pipes from tanks containing liquids with flash points below or equal to $100^{\circ}\mathrm{F}$ with tanks containing liquids with flash points above $100^{\circ}\mathrm{F}$ .  
- Protect low-pressure storage tanks interconnected with fume recovery or collection systems against explosion propagation if they normally contain ignitable mixtures and ignition sources (e.g., spontaneous heating) could be or are normally present (continuous flames as in flares, thermal oxidizers, etc.), by one of the following methods:

a. Oxidant concentration reduction (e.g., inerting or purging). This method should not be used if open manhole activities, such as sampling and liquid or solids addition, are performed. Inerted systems should be monitored by oxygen analyzers, with high $\mathrm{O}_2$ alarms, and a backup inert gas (usually nitrogen) supply (e.g., nitrogen cylinders) hooked up to the manifold and automatically activated by the high-level oxygen concentration in the analyzer.

b. Combustible vapor concentration reduction (e.g., ventilation). This method is not often used in industry.

c. Explosion isolation (detonation arresters). This method is widely used in the chemical process industries. Where an explosion isolation system is used, the detonation arresters must be approved by testing by accepted test methods. In this system, there must be a detonation arrester at each tank vent nozzle interconnected with the vent manifold and a detonation flame arrester at the end of the manifold immediately upstream of the

feed nozzle of any vapor control equipment, such as a thermal oxidizer or a scrubber (Fig. 2). Detonation arresters should be provided with temperature sensors on each side and as close as possible to the face of the arresting element. Instrument the arrester to automatically close valves or initiate other actions that will eliminate the possibility of a stabilized flame burning on the arrester element. Do not locate the sensor in a thermowell that will delay its response. If the sensor is to be a metal-sheathed thermocouple, it must be of small diameter, for example, $\frac{1}{4}$ in. (6 mm), and must be inserted through a suitable packing gland. (Note: Detonation arresters may not be suitable in systems where powders are added to the tank on a regular basis as the arresters could become plugged and fail to handle normal in-and-out breathing.)

## 2. Pumps

Many types of pumps are used for transferring flammable and combustible liquids, such as centrifugal and positive-displacement (rotary, reciprocating, and diaphragm) pumps.

In recent years, sealless pumps are being used more frequently for highly flammable or toxic liquids. Both centrifugal and rotary pumps are available in sealless designs.

Centrifugal pumps. Centrifugal pumps are the most commonly used type of pump in chemical and pharmaceutical plants. For flammable and combustible liquids, they should be provided with double inside mechanical seals or tandem mechanical seals with a compatible buffer liquid between the seals. The following hazards can occur with centrifugal pumps:

■ Mechanical seal failure  
■ Reduced or low flow  
■ Overpressurization from thermal expansion  
■ High temperatures  
■ Cavitation

The following preventive and protective measures should be provided for the above-mentioned hazards:

Mechanical seal failure. Mechanical seal failure can occur due to loss of buffer fluid through the seal. This can be avoided by providing a seal system that consists of a circulating pressurized reservoir with an internal cooling coil and low-level alarm. Figure 3 is a schematic of such a system.

In recent years, gas-barrier seals have been used in place of buffer-liquid seals in many applications, and they warrant serious consideration.

Reduced or low flow. Operation of centrifugal pumps at severely reduced flow or at shutoff should be avoided, as this can create unstable flow conditions and cause excessive vibration and damage to pumps, drivers, couplings, gears, piping, and adjacent equipment. To prevent this from happening, centrifugal pumps should be provided with a minimum-flow recirculation (bypass) line from the pump discharge side of the pump, located upstream of the block and check valves. This bypass line should preferably be routed back to the pump supply vessel if it is an adequate "heat sink." Otherwise, the minimum-flow bypass line should be routed through an external cooler before returning to the supply vessel or pump suction line.

Overpressurization from thermal expansion. Overpressurization protection from thermal expansion is needed for centrifugal pumps that can be valved in when there is a remote start-up capability or when both the suction and the discharge lines have automatic closing valves.

Overpressurization protection is also needed if the pump shutoff head exceeds the pressure rating of the system piping. Protection is usually provided by a pressure relief valve from which the discharge is directed back to the pump supply vessel.

High temperature. High temperature can occur in a liquid when a pump operates against a closed valve, which is known as "deadheading." This is a

very dangerous situation for some liquids that can decompose when subjected to high temperatures and, subsequently, may lead to an explosion within the pump. When pumping such high-hazard liquids, a temperature sensor should be installed in the pump casing that will alarm and shut the motor down before a thermal runaway reaction can occur.

Cavitation. Cavitation can cause pressure variation, shaft deflection, vibration, or mechanical shock that will damage mechanical seal components. This problem can be avoided by proper design of supply vessels and suction piping systems. It can usually be avoided by ensuring that the pump has adequate net-positive suction head (NPSH) and that entrained gases are avoided. NPSH requirements should always be obtained from the pump manufacturer.

Backflow can occur in a centrifugal pump system when the motor (or other driver) is stopped, either intentionally or accidentally. This can result in backflow of liquid through the pump to the suction vessel and, possibly, vessel overflow. It may also result in reverse rotation of a non-running installed standby spare pump, which could damage it. To avoid or limit backflow, a check valve should be installed in the pump discharge line. For highly flammable liquids, it may be desirable to install two check valves in series. Alternatively, a fast-acting open-shut valve, activated by a low-pressure sensor in the discharge line that will shut the valve tightly, can be used. When check valves or fast-acting valves are installed in the discharge line of a pump, it may be necessary to establish a way to prevent hydraulic hammer.

Positive-displacement pumps. Most positive-displacement pumps require a pressure relief device to protect against overpressure that can be caused by a closed discharge valve or restricted discharge line. External pressure relief valves are typically used because they are easy to see when open, can easily be adjusted to any set pressure, and remain open only long enough to relieve pressure, thereby reducing process liquid loss. Some positive-displacement pumps are sometimes provided with an internal relief valve, but this is a drawback because an internal relief valve does not dissipate heat. Even if a pump comes equipped with an internal relief valve, it is strongly recommended that an external relief valve also be installed. The external relief valve should be located as close as possible to the pump discharge nozzle and before any block valve.

Diaphragm pumps are used for many hazardous-liquid (flammable and toxic) handling applications because they have no mechanical seals or packing and are essentially sealless pumps. They can be either motor driven or air operated and can have either single or double diaphragms.

Since single-diaphragm pumps can leak if the diaphragm fails, double-diaphragm pumps are recommended for highly hazardous liquids. The space between the two diaphragms can be fitted with any of several leak-detection sensors (using a pH, liquid conductivity, or pressure sensor), which will detect a leak in the inner diaphragm and set off an alarm.

Air-operated diaphragm pumps usually vibrate because of their mode of operation. Therefore, the suction and discharge piping should not be hard piped to the pump nozzles, but should have flexible hose piping.
