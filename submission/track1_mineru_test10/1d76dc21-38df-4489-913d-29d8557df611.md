think we can't. And part of that skepticism is well-founded. Let's be honest: we have a spotty track record. Some argue that architects and planners haven't contributed much to solving our cities' problems - even that they've been part of them.

Earlier this week a letter appeared in the Pittsburgh Press. It was written by a citizen from Carnegie. "Mon Valley Blitz Is Hogwash," the headline read. Let me quote a few sentences: "A 17-member team of outside 'experts' started Thursday to blitz the Mon Valley for the grand total of five days...and then presto, change-o, alakazoo, they will come up with how to save the [whole] economically depressed area. What utter hogwash!" The writer adds. "There is a much stronger word, but the Pittsburgh Press is a family newspaper." Well, that's certainly putting it on the line. But does he have it right? If the Mon Valley Regional Urban Design Assistance Team, and if this conference, is simply a quick fly-by of outsiders spray-applying so-called "solutions" left and right, then "hogwash" truly is too polite a word.

Let me suggest that something quite different is going on here. That difference has to do with our attitude toward the people that we're serving.

Picture architects and urban designers at their drawing boards (or computers, these days). When they move the pencil (or cursor), they seldom if ever have total control. There's more than one hand guiding the design process. The men and women entrusted by the community to design the places in which our citizens live, work, play, pray, or learn have many hands tugging at the pencil - the hands of developers, zoning officials, and bankers, to name a few. What we should be trying to do here is to get the public's hand on that pencil. That's what happened last Monday night in Homestead. The real action wasn't on stage where all the drawings were laid out. The real action was in the minds of the audience members, engaged in shaping their own dreams, using architects and designers as resources.

Housewives, steelworkers, teachers, priests, students, and more listened - and they talked back. Some ideas they liked; some they didn't. But the point is: they were talking architecture, design, urban planning. They were participating in, and influencing, the creative process by their involvement. Imagine that: nonprofessionals daring to question, to challenge, to advise, to inform. It's revolutionary! And it says to me that there is hope.

We architects cannot and should not impose our dreams on the public. We do not serve by dreaming our private dreams; we serve by dreaming the dreams of the community. For that [sharing of dreams] to be possible, we need the public - as sponsors, clients, and allies.

What kind of architect is able to this? Not the egoist sort that Ayn Rand wrote about in "The Fountainhead." We've had too many architects and urban designers who imposed their visions on communities, and communities that were made worse on account of it. That's not the kind of design professional we need or mean to encourage at this conference. Down with the masterbuilder, and up with partnership. That's the real key to the future.

To get to the future from where we are now, we must make room for, and nurture, what I call the "citizen architect." What does this citizen architect look like? This person is committed to universal enfranchisement, and works to see that everyone in the community is given a meaningful stake in, and a part in directing, the future. The citizen architect is committed to seeing that, at the drafting table, the public's hand exerts at least as much force as the developer's or banker's.

The practice of architecture must no longer be seen as a luxury that only the wealthy can afford. The public must be a vital part of the process. Architecture is the most public of the arts. It should be collaborative.

The challenge for architects is to get out of their offices and into the community. The challenge for the community is to stop waiting for a white knight, be it the federal government or a master builder. Community people should take charge of the future by engaging architects and urban designers to go to work for them.

In the final analysis, architects and planners need the public more than the public needs us. Unless the hands of our neighbors are on our pencils, our art will always be on the periphery of community life. We will be on some occasions admirable, but largely irrelevant.

Nearly a half-century ago, a great architect was asked by the citizens of Pittsburgh what they should do to turn their city back from what seemed like an inevitable economic, environmental, and human catastrophe. His answer was simple and direct: "Abandon it." Fortunately, Pittsburgh got a second opinion. It listened to the pride and confidence of its own people. Frank LLOYd Wright missed an incredible opportunity.

If the huge amount of publicity generated by the Mon Valley R/UDAT and the Remaking Cities Conference is any indication of interest, you've got the public's ear. All you have to do in days and years ahead is to win people's hearts.

The first was in London in November 1986, when over 1,000 people, many of them from the very community groups that Ted Pappas has talked about, attended for the first time, in a dialogue with architects who were committing themselves to the community architecture approach. I think you here should acknowledge that we are beginning now to have a regular conference - all credit to David Lewis for bringing it to Pittsburgh this time, and who knows where it will be next time. And the man who gave the keynote speech then will be here with you Friday.

Cities are changing. Britain is no different from the rest of Europe, and Europe is very similar to the United States. The reasons cities were built has now gone. Liverpool, a major port in Europe, became famous because it had a seaboard facing the United States. Very little maritime traffic now comes out of or into Liverpool. Manchester was a major financial center; it too has lost its way and needs to be revitalized. Birmingham was one of the world's major small-engineering industrial towns. Glasgow, Belfast, and Cardiff, all capitals of their own countries, are completely different now, from an industrial point of view. They have lost the major industries they became successful on.

Some cities aren't surviving. They are not even getting by. The amount paid for people on unemployment benefit is not earned by the rest of the people in the city. Liverpool has a working population of only 25 percent. It cannot sustain the other 75 percent without major subsidy from outside.

Some people would argue that you shouldn't remake a city if it's lost it's reason in life. It should decline. After all, history books are full of declining cities. But we miss a point if we think that's the simple way out. I would argue that there is a resource in the world's major industrial cities: that is, ordinary people. This resource, if tapped properly, and seen as an opportunity, not as a problem, perhaps could provide the renaissance we need.

We look to big systems. We look to single industries - steel, nuclear power, coal, North Sea oil - as reasons for prosperity. But they don't need that much labor anymore. We've still got millions of people waiting to be given new opportunities in life. If we therefore consider labor to be the very resource that regenerates cities from within, then we in Europe, Britain in particular, and the cities in the United States might have a new way of looking at regeneration: tapping the resource of human endeavor.

The decline, therefore, of traditional industries, which does lead to major shifts of population, and leaves behind the infirm, the elderly, the unemployed, or the people who simply don't want to move shouldn't distract us from the fact that we have to provide a service for those who remain in these cities. The challenge is there. Architects have to play a role. We have to play perhaps an enabling role, to spur things to happen. Our skill shouldn't be simply providing buildings. It is the means of providing those buildings as well, and how the community itself can generate wealth from within. The top-down approach is not going to be the solution for European cities of the future. Ordinary people are waiting to be given the opportunity to participate. They tend to be ignored if we plan from outside.

There are so many initiatives being promoted now in every country - in Britain alone. I'll list you some of the programs government is producing from the top down. The urban programs include all sorts of subsidies, such as urban development grants, urban regeneration grants, urban development corporations, derelict land grants, enterprise zones, simplified planning zones, estate action. All these are fine. Credit must go to the draftsmen and draftswomen who actually write the new rules - why not? There are incentives to give young people new training: youth enterprise schemes, youth training programs, community programs, restart programs, local enterprise agencies, small firm advice, enterprise allowance schemes, and adult training programs. All good stuff, but basically not getting anywhere. The reason is, ordinary people find the bureaucracy involved in participating beyond their understanding. Perhaps another role for the architects and urban planners: to articulate their needs and interpret the rules, and bring these rules in understandable form to these people.

The problem in the top-down approach, which, as I said, is well meant, is that it's still geared within the old-fashioned vertical hierarchies of the ministries in Europe, particularly in Britain. Housing (a major problem in the United Kingdom because of the mass solutions provided

in the 60 s that weren't appropriate) is under the control of what we call the Department of the Environment. Some cities have massive unemployment problems. Burnley, where I work, has up to 60 percent unemployment on one of its estates. That problem is in another department, the Department of Employment. Other departments involved in the inner city include the Home Office, Crime and Policing, Education, Health, and Social Security. Six or seven ministries have responsibility for cities.

Prime Minister Thatcher sat for eight months as chairperson bringing these ministries together, and she's now appointed - and this is a good start - Kenneth Clark as the minister in charge of the inner-city problems. But he has problems, too. I haven't mentioned the Treasury. The Treasury has much to learn, particularly from the experiences you've had in the United States.

We have a slow catastrophic problem to resolve. In Mexico, they had an earthquake three years ago, and they had an instant catastrophic problem. They also had vertical hierarchies, and for six to eight months, nothing was being done. A million people were on the streets saying government was not looking after their interests. And it was true, until the President then decided, "We have to bring together from each ministry the best brains into one ministry to resolve the building of 50,000 houses in a short period."

It was done in Mexico, not the richest country in the world. It can therefore be done in countries that are richer, with hard currencies, like Britain. In order to show by example, offices in Mexico were set up in the streets. Architects worked from the locality. They weren't remote, and they were available 24 hours a day. They were also there to interpret not just the housing needs of people, but where possible, self-build was encouraged, and ordinary people could see - because temporary housing was provided in the streets - their permanent homes being built around them. It took away the stress.

Imaginative tax incentives are a must. The private sector cannot possibly be expected to take unlimited risk without any partnership in that risk by government. Tax breaks must be part of that partnership.

But most important of all, having resolved that, local people must see that they can benefit from wealth creation. It's ironic that we in Britain spend \$18 billion on administering the poor. We have two and a half million people unemployed. We lose tax because they're not working: \$5 billion is lost in tax. And we pay out the rest in social security payments, and other dole payments, simply to keep people poor. We have an infrastructure: two million people in the country administering poverty.

I once went into a part of Lancashire - and people know the sort of formula I was advocating. I said that if we were allowed to have our head in this area for four years, we would make sure there would be no unemployed in this town. That's the formula, if we work carefully with local authority. The Department of Health and Social Security managing director called me in very quickly, panicking, saying, "Rod, you're an enemy of the people. I've got 42 people in this office, all with mortgages, all with kids at school. They administer the poor. If you solve the unemployment problem, what do I do with my staff?"

I said to him. "Read the 1969 Housing Act. Up until 1969 we were knocking houses down. We've knocked down something like 5 million houses because they were unfit for human habitation." These are properties mainly pre-1919. The Public Health Inspectors panicked when they read the legislation of 1969. Their jobs were going to be on the line because there was going to be very little, if any, demolition of older properties. We reversed our housing policy. We'd seen some of the problems with the new solutions, and they simply weren't getting rid of our housing problem.

But whoever drafted that legislation was very clever. They said the people who will give improvement grants to save older property will be the public health inspectors. So I think the very people now who are administering poverty could, with a shift of emphasis, administer wealth creation.

My final points therefore are that we have the labor. We have all these people simply sitting, doing nothing. They're not contributing. It's a waste. They're a much better value than any product we could make in a factory or dig out of the earth. We must tap them as a resource. There are examples throughout Europe of successes, not on a great scale. There are people in this room who have done that, and they'll be telling you their stories this week. I think government will make things happen if it encourages partnership, bringing in the private sector to commit itself to wealth creation, but only if ordinary people are allowed to enjoy that wealth creation by benefitting in material gain.

What better incentive, I would argue, for avoiding vandalism and crime. Who is going to destroy a community if they help make it? What's wrong with self-build? People say the unions won't allow it. The unions will allow it, because at the moment there is a boom in Europe now, particularly in Britain. You simply can't get builders to do the work for you. Architects have got so much work on, they're looking to foreign practices to subcontract work. We have an opportunity to broaden our influence as professionals by allowing these people to participate in a new renaissance. I believe we've come to a crossroads, and as was said by Ted Pappas, we've come to a point where we need friends. What better friends can we make than by persuading people that we're worthwhile? People will always pay architects' fees if we create the wealth for them. They hate paying if we don't create wealth.

So a partnership of enterprise is what I'm calling for, a partnership between builders, professionals, and politicians - local, state, and national. And most important of all, with ordinary people - the people who count, local people, who at the moment aren't seen as part of any formula for success. But we must make sure they are.

This conference starts off on the right foot. Four days from now, we'll see if we've contributed to new solutions. The mood is already right from what we've heard up to now. I must say that the Royal Institute of British Architects is proud to co-convene, with our friends from the American Institute of Architects, this conference. Let us, therefore, jointly go forward with enthusiasm and optimism to strive for the success that many people are hoping we can achieve by Saturday.

## NORMAN E. WAKEFIELD

Executive Chairman, Y. J. Lovell (Holdings) PLC

Some 17 years ago I stood not too far from here to express my views - uninformed and inexperienced as they were then - on the complex and vital issues involved in the renewal of our cities. It was, in fact, at the 1970 meeting of the American Association for the Advancement of Science in Chicago. In those days concern about city problems, born of the decay of urban life, which had already driven some hard lessons home in the United States, had barely touched our consciousness in the U.K. The problems in our cities were still measured in terms of slum clearance, that public sector version of vandalism on the shape of the bulldozer, labeled as a social service, subsidized, non-profit-making - indeed huge loss-making - and therefore respectable.

The renaissance of Baltimore and Boston, London Docklands and Glasgow were but gleams in the eye, the visions and hopes of a few people. We knew then, as we know now, the nature and the extent of the problems. We knew all about the bits of the city. There were excellent analyses, such as Norton Long's "The Unwalled City," Jane Jacobs's "The Death and Life of Great American Cities," and Vance Packard's "A Nation of Strangers," and all helped extend our knowledge of why our cities no longer worked. They provided important and interesting insights into the factors at the root of the breakdown of urban communities and reaffirmed what we could all see a0round us but had become conditioned to, the obvious dereliction in once-great cities. We understood all the bits: what we could not comprehend was the whole. What was missing was a process that could weld together all the vested interests, all the conflicting views, all the available resources into a common, creative, and positive sense of purpose. In the United Kingdom, where we had been conditioned to government intervention and involvement in almost every area of our lives - the Planning regime, New Towns movement, massive public sector housing programs - it was comfortable to throw the problems back to them. "When are you going to do something about the cities?" was the clarion call. The private sector, molded into a conformist role as a consequence, was not able or ready, as the frontier of government involvement drew back, to fill this gap.

If, as two countries, we come at these problems - for understandable cultural and political reasons - from different directions, then I suspect, as a practitioner in both, that we have a lot to gain from each other's experiences and opportunities to meet somewhere in the middle.

Now down to business, I shall not please all the audience here today by saying that the solutions have much more to do with entrepreneurial attitudes and activities and meetings of mind than they do with physical planning or design - much more to do with bringing architecture back to the people and away from the arrogance of the "human filing cabinet" approach which has characterized so much of the monumental styles in post-war development.

I lived alongside the Baltimore renaissance. It was a great urban partnership between the entrepreneurial and political, a victory of dreams and determination, which has provided a model and inspired many cities on both sides of the Atlantic to re-examine the possibilities and expectations and encourage self-initiated remedies, imposed neither from outside not above. Baltimore began to destroy the myth that the forces that created our cities in the first place were now somehow impotent in facing the task of 20th-century renewal. It gave lie to the belief that the flair, the enterprise, and the dynamic of individuals had somehow to be subordinated to public servants if we were to fulfill any goals other than short-term commercial ones.

Equally, it was clear that private enterprise could not go it alone. Urban dereliction brings negative values, local hostility, and suspicion, and destroys hope and belief that our cities can ever be good places to live or work in again. Baltimore signaled to us that an inspired and dedicated public service can work productively and creatively with the private sector and that the real conflicts of interest are man-made.

Out of this was born in the U.K., long before London Docklands or urban development corporations or "community architecture" came on the scene, a partnership movement, a voluntary joining of forces - with the private sector providing development, marketing, financing, and construction services; using public sector land assets at a discounted price; and raising pump-priming finance from government grant programs geared to venture capital from private sources.

The critics and opponents said it would not work. Those on the right said that left-wing councils would never sit around the table with the private sector; that institutionalized solutions, such as urban development corporations, would be the only way to knock sense into autocratic authorities. Those on the left said that private enterprise would never care for people, that it only sought out market opportunities that were immediately do-able, leaving the rest abandoned or to be picked up at the expense of the public purse.

Neither was right. Whilst a number of urban development corporations have been established across the land and the London Docklands is a success story that even British understatement cannot deny, the real longterm change is the private-private partnerships springing up across the land (a pooling rather than a polarization of resources), producing results unachievable in any other way. Enlightened private enterprise seeing new markets to serve, and positive-thinking public authorities seeking to harness this capability for the common good.

Amongst its other activities, my company has specialized in partnership-working for over 10 years, initially to create affordable housing. Moving progressively from green field sites into the urban areas, we have been able to make an increasing contribution and have worked with over 70 local authorities in mostly housing-led initiatives but increasingly into larger-scale mixed-use developments to breathe life back into urban areas.

This experience has led us toward a more ambitious goal to provide the missing link: the creation of a private sector urban process which brings together in trust and harmony all the resources and all the interests that have a legitimate voice in the exciting and realizable task of city renewal.

Whilst there are a few cities which have outlived their relevance, most are still major conurbations in their own right where people would choose to live and work given an attractive urban environment. It is all these issues which we have sought to address with an organization which is profit-making called P.R.O.B.E., an acronym for Partnership Renewal of the Built Environment. This organization involves, organizes, and orchestrates all the interests, inputs, and resources necessary. It is an enabling mechanism which starts from the belief that incompatibility between public and private sectors is not fundamental and that we are serving a market, the product of which is urban environment. We are pragmatic, and no urban problem is too big or too small. We learn to communicate in bureaucrats' language. We unashamedly extract from the public purse the maximum of grants, usually on a gearing of around \$1 of government money to \$5 of private. We form non-profit-making "development trusts," where the public sector pledges the land or worn-out housing - probably of no value when we start. Enlightened banks and building societies lend money against these assets to the trust company, which we manage, anticipating as covenant the added values our efforts will create.

I will not pretend that we have cracked every problem nor that we always succeed. Urban renewal will not exorcise poverty or unemployment, and self-sustaining activity, once the builder has moved out, is a vital element of the complicated formula. We are tackling, head-on, deep-rooted them-and-us attitudes; we are a conduit for business involvement and investment; and we are providing sounding boards for local people to debate, argue, and ultimately co-operate. And in this way, I believe, we can remove the despair, desolation, and isolation that are the hallmarks of so many inner-city areas.

Now all this would be just words and talk but for the fact that there has been much exciting progress over the past few years. If our involvements and commitments are anything to go by, this pace is set to accelerate over the next few years. There is every good chance, in my view, of making sufficient impact on the problem on a national scale during the life of the current Government to demon-

strate meaningfully to all our citizens that the partnership approach can really work.

I want in a moment to walk you through one or two of our urban projects in the U.K., where, as you will see from our banner headlines, we rather modestly claim to be something of market leaders: "Leadership in Partnership." But before doing so, I would like to sketch the real-life scenario in which we operate and the forces we seek to harness in the renewal process. These are typical, but differ in weighting and precedence from place to place.

First, there are the cries for help which manifest themselves in crime, vandalism, graffiti, and all the other detritus of physical urban dereliction. The voices are hostile and bitter, demanding to know with increasing urgency when somebody is going to do something for them.

Second, there are the local authorities, both elected members and professional officers, pampered in the past by successive governments of every political persuasion whose only solution was to throw money at the problem - now in a country where good national housekeeping is the order of the day, when public finance is no longer freely available, and do-it-yourself is the flavor of the month.

Third, there is local business and its leaders, preoccupied with their own problems of making a living but increasingly aware that a healthy and profitable business can only operate long-term if it is contributing to a healthy and profitable environment.

Fourth, there is Government claiming to have created a healthy economic climate which enables the rest of us to invest and get to work on the problems which, on the strength of the Prime Minister's statements, is amongst our top national priorities.

Fifth, the development and construction industry, conditioned by years of public patronage, for a long time waiting impotently in the wings; now having fame, or at least responsibility thrust upon it - and dipping its toe tentatively in the water.

Finally, outside the immediate urban arena and largely insulated from it are the financial institutions, busy creating national wealth, increasingly uneasy about the urban decay so evident around them but concerned, and rightly so, about the risk and return on their investments. After all, they are but the custodians of your money and mine.

Into this vacuum of doubt are arriving good men and true, fired by the very enterprise and initiative that created the cities in the first place; and rejecting the outmoded notion that private enterprise can only be rapacious and that the public sector was the custodian of the public good and, above all, never the twain shall meet. The kind of partnership I describe is emerging all over the country - voluntary development agencies, the Phoenix Initiative, Business in the Community, and the construction and development industry's ventures such as our own - willing to assume this creative role. Authorities and local people alike are beginning to market the potential of their city, rather than frightening investors away. All this proffers a realizable goal: that over the next 15 years or so, the complete renewal of our cities as good places in which to live, work, shop, and play could be within our grasp.

Our approach through P.R.O.B.E. is not to offer pre-ordained solutions but to ask questions, to canvass views and opinions, to talk in terms of a profitable city - a new word and concept for so many who are so deeply engrained with the belief that a surplus of value over cost cannot be to the public benefit. We talk about creating demand, and therefore value, recycling profits, eliminating negative values, trading off some of the easy to deal with the more difficult. Our development-evaluation computer programs can throw up as many alternatives as fast as we can feed in the variables. Each situation is unique, requiring different responses, needing public sector contribution on different gearings and for different reasons - to make viable, to beautify, to provide a quality statement, a vote of confidence in the future, or perhaps some immediate environmental impact.

Let me touch upon one or two of the kind of things that we are doing.

I said at the beginning that most of our earlier initiatives were housing-led. You will see some of the initiatives on this map. These represent a groundswell of involvement and knowledge, and will create opportunities and results in a shorter period than most people realize.

Now, sometimes when we get a successful project, everybody says, "That looks easy; can't see what the problem was." This is a picture of the edge of Bristol Docks as it was, vandalized and occupied by high-rise buildings left from a previous era. Dereliction and pollution of every sort; and a wasting asset, the water, which was a liability to the city when it should have been an environmental and financial asset.

We created, three or four years ago, some mixed development along the water - without any public-sector support. We were out to demonstrate several things. We wanted to show that if we invested money in the infrastructure, and created amenities, we could create value; that we could recycle some of the profit to affordable housing and to rental housing of specialized kinds - recycling these values within the totality of one project.

We built a marina, because we believed that that would create activity, value, and amenity, and of course it did. We took a site in Swansea Docks which really was a mess. We couldn't make this one work, and we therefore had to have public-sector investment in the marine works to reinstate the land, which is on the edge of a bay that is very heavily exposed to winds and storms. Again we were able to demonstrate that to a depressed area of the country we could bring investment, and demand, and a good place to live, and affordable housing.

We've also played a big part in London Docklands. Every city has water of some kind - lake, canal, river, or sea - and the trick is, by attractive landscaping and environmental treatment, to make that an asset for the people who live there in environmental terms.

That was the easy end. We were cutting our teeth three or four years ago. We were treading the unknown; we didn't know whether we were going to make a profit or even a living out of it. Whether the risks would overwhelm the rewards, or whether the delicate balance between public sector and private sector investment would actually work. And it did. So we thought, then, it was time to venture forth into areas of the country which are economically more deprived, perhaps, than London Docklands, or Bristol or Swansea.

We headed north. We felt that if we could create some landmarks - significant landmarks on the urban scene, in the northern part of the country, using imaginatively and ingeniously the public-sector grant regime which is available and has been constantly underspent over the last three years - that we could show something really could be done.

This is Salford, which is the poor man's part of Manchester. There were docks hidden and lost in the debris and dereliction. Public sector put in £25 million. We've just begun to create a total environment which starts with housing, a hotel, and commercial and industrial development in an area which is probably as bad as any in the country. This has to be a model, because we're just getting underway. In creating this focus, we've drawn interest and we have waiting lists for housing in an area where we were told we would not be able to sell or rent anything.

People argue that it's easy to build new housing. What about the old mills that Rod Hackney talked about, the old warehouses - our inheritance from the Victorian era? What could we do with them? Again, in the Bristol Docks, we couldn't make them work economically, initially. The spans were all wrong; the structures were all suspect; the water had eroded the foundations, and so on. We didn't let that discourage us. We went scavenging for grants. We got a grant from English Heritage and other sources, till we were at the point where, by using this as gap financing, we could finance the whole project. And we started work.

This is a model, but I promise you it does look like that. And the interesting thing is, it's drawing people back into the city; it's drawing purchasing power, and success, back into the city.

That's only the beginning of the story. We realize, of course, that all of what we were doing was pepper-potting large areas of urban desolation. We felt that we were saying something, demonstrating something, but we realize that we weren't really tackling the renewal of a city on any scale. And so we pushed as fast as our knowledge and our experience would allow into area-based projects, where we need substantial public-sector front-money grants, and where we're deliberately opening up into open space.

A different and interesting site to tackle, 100-odd acres in perhaps the most difficult part of Britain, because there is no water, is west of Birmingham. It's covered with derelict steel works and slag heaps, and its only advantage is its location to the motorway. We have built a mixed-use development and we're already getting inundated with demands for supermarket space and for light-industrial. And a familiar name to you. For the first time, we've begun to see all the elements of the city - leisure, commerce, housing, industry - coming together. The interesting thing about this site is that in its heyday, 3,500 people were employed on that 100 acres. Today, it's less than 1,000. In two years' time, when that development is complete, we should be back to 2,500 permanent jobs.

Our latest project, one of a number, is in Blackburn, in the north of the country. Again it's an area-based venture. We're only in the conceptual stage. We set up a development trust with the City Council, P.R.O.B.E. (our organization), and the Department of the Environment as partners. We certainly create development profit at that level. Those profits are shared: 60 percent to the City, 20 percent to us, and 20 percent to the D.O.E. We have a partners' agreement that we'll recycle that back into the city.

At first sight, another disused canal, derelict housing, underutilized large assets - a wasteland. A statement of the failure of our modern society and modern age. A waterway which is nothing but a liability. It's a danger, it's unattractive, it's costly. There's a message going through all our experiences that no country these days can afford the cost of urban dereliction. And yet from time to time where somebody, a community group, or some self-initiated project has gone to work - in this case, on one of the canal locks - you can just get the first feel of promise and prospect. And so we've taken on, with the help of a \$5.5 million-Urban Regeneration Grant, the whole of this area, 70 acres in all. It's destroyed itself over the last 25 years. We've been at work on it for six months. We've got all the necessary planning approvals. We've done deals with a hotel group, an ice rink, about 800 houses (not only market-rate housing, and affordable housing, but also self-help group, as well). We should be breaking ground within the next six months.

The lesson for all of us is that it is possible to reverse urban decay faster than it's occurred.

The important point that I make is: What are liabilities in the relics of the industrial age - these waterways - can be turned into such attractive assets around which development activity can grow.

May I conclude by summing up what I believe to be the key signposts for the future:

First, enough progress has been made on the ground for us to have the confidence that the problems are within our power to solve. We can reject the debilitating preoccupation with the myths and realities of our urban problems, fragmented ownership and interests, confusion of public responsibility, all the social and economic aspects, so confused and confusing that they lead to the feeling that the grim, worn-out inner city cannot ever again be a good place to live and work.

Second, we can take heart in the surge of initiatives, institutional and individual, which are, as we sit here

Fourth, and most important, the key lies in public-private partnerships, with the marrying of the experience, the skills, and the resources of each. The organized capacity to produce good new communities and work places within our urban areas is evolving. ## RICHARD NATHAN

Professor of Public and International Affairs, Woodrow Wilson School, Princeton University
Cities in the 80's: Change and Challenge

I'm honored to be here to talk about remaking cities, a task that many people in this audience have labored at. My message is a somber one. I want to set the scene and give you some perspective, particularly from the American point of view, as we look at our cities and their conditions.

I spent this past weekend at the Wingspread Conference Center in Wisconsin, where a group convened to commemorate the 20th anniversary of the report of the National Commission on Civil Disorders, the so-called Kerner Commission, which 20 years ago was created by Lyndon Johnson to study the riot conditions in the nation's cities. What are the conditions 20 years after the Kerner Commission Report? What can we say today about American cities?

Some of you may remember the Commission Report; it had in it the statement: "America is fast moving toward two nations, one white and one black, separate and unequal." The Commission said the underlying reason for the problems in the cities that erupted in that period was racism. I was a member of the staff of the Commission and worked on the proposals that the Commission made for jobs, housing, education, welfare, and urban policy. Twenty years later, as we looked at the city in America, we asked ourselves: "Is the Commission diagnosis still right? Are we still moving toward two nations?"

The consensus of the people who were at the meeting in Wisconsin, and much of the discussion that has been going on over the last several weeks, is that the conditions have changed.

I want to focus on the changed way we diagnose the conditions and problems of our cities, and the changed prescription that is emerging.

What is different 20 years after the Kerner Commission Report?

The single word that captures the change - a term that has been widely discussed among people who care about large cities in America - is underclass. The term has become an encoded reference to the problems concentrated particularly among minority citizens in large cities. What is happening is that a bifurcation is now developing within the urban minority community. The problems of the American "hidden city" are becoming worse at precisely the time, in terms of national policy, that we're doing less about them.

The reason for this is shown by the theory put forward by William Julius Wilson in "The Truly Disadvantaged." Wilson's theory is that the role models are now able to move out of the city. In this respect, the new bifurcation of American cities is a function of success. It is a result in part of the civil rights revolution, which has opened up opportunities for members of minority groups who care and work hard and want to move out of the ghetto.

At Princeton we studied America's 50 largest cities as they were during the decade of the 70's. In the 1970's those cities lost 5 percent of their population. But the poverty population of the 50 largest cities grew by 12 percent. And the concentrated poverty, the percent of the population that lived in census tracts with more than 20 percent poor people, grew by 30.5 percent. The white poverty population declined by 18 percent, and the black

If we look at what the Urban Institute experts currently define as "the underclass," we see this group concentrated in the "hidden city," areas with chronic and seemingly-intractable social problems, including unemployment, drugs, and crime, the places where many middle-class citizens believe they can't and shouldn't go. That population, according to an Urban Institute study just released, was 752,000 in 1970 and grew to over 2.5 million - an increase of 230 percent.

Professor Wilson's theory, I believe, holds up.

In the 70's, something else happened in America. Another study by two economists at the Urban Institute, Scott McKinney and Ann B. The study said: "The redistribution of the white population toward more integrated neighborhoods gathered steam in the 70's, and a significant proportion of the black population shifted away from established ghetto areas. So you have two things - this pulling apart, the hardening of the core city problem, and a spreading out and bifurcation of the minority population. If you look back 20 years at the Kerner Commission Report, the answer is that the problem is different and more complex now. This bifurcation phenomenon needs to be understood if we are going to do something about the hard problems of our cities.

The Pittsburgh story, thanks to wonderful people like Jack Robin, is better. For the top ten cities in the United States in the 70's, their poverty population grew by 19 percent, but Pittsburgh went against the trend. The poverty population grew by only about one percent. I have more news for you - not news I am happy to bring. We did one study of Central Business Districts. We tried to determine whether any of the changes in the neighborhoods downtown affected the numbers and conditions of the impacted populations of the inner cities. Did downtown commercial development projects affect the underclass or the essential makeup of the city in any way? What we've found is there isn't a relationship we can see at this time. It's wonderful what's been done in Baltimore, and in Boston, and in downtown Philadelphia. But the figures, I regret to tell you, do not yet show a relationship between downtown development and genuine urban revival.

I want to devote a few minutes to the question: What do we do about this situation? Taking into account the retrenchment in Washington, the conservatism of the country, the turning away from this agenda, what can we say about how we should respond?

I have a strong belief about one area in which we should change our approach. We give too much attention in the policy process to narrow interests. Somebody wants to have a program and put their name on it and pass a bill and say, "Hey, look at that!" What we are learning in the 80's is the importance of asking: What works? How can institutions change? We need to look more closely at systems and institutions. In the past, we have not given enough attention in the public sector to putting policy into effect I believe we need to do this and I see some encouraging things in the policy arena that suggest we are.

Despite the fact that the federal government is pulling back from the cities, state governments are doing more. The rising role of the states is one story of the 80's. The states are doing more in the areas of health, education, welfare, and infrastructure to create a labor force and a climate for economic development.

One area that I want to discuss briefly is the area under the heading of what can be called "new-style workfare." State governments are doing things in quiet but very important ways to change welfare systems from being payment systems to being systems that provide education, training, work experience, child care, and jobs to reach into this urban underclass in order to save people. These interventions are not easy. Institutional change is being attempted under the GAIN [Greater Avenues for Independence] program in California, Governor Dukakis's ET [Employment and Training Choices] program in Massachusetts, and Governor Thomas Kean's REACH [Realizing Economic Achievement] program in New Jersey. Two-thirds of the states are now reforming welfare to get people off the dole and into the labor force.

We need to change systems of government in ways that can affect these hardened problems that characterize the 80's on the American urban scene. We need to do this in the schools. The greatest failure of urban institutions is the failure of urban schools. You all know all of the stories that go with that. In effect these "new-style workfare" programs, unfortunately, are doing a job the schools should have done. But some schools too are responding to the challenge.

The American Enterprise Institute recently issued a report about the new consensus in American domestic policy. It is based on a bargain. The bargain can be seen in welfare and in education. It works like this: If you are in a new service program, you are required to participate in whatever the services are - remediation, job search, training. But in exchange for this, the state is obligated to provide money for these services. In California they have appropriated \$500 million - half a billion dollars - to the GAIN program. That's a lot of money. In sum, there are some things that are happening that look at systems and institutions and at the kinds of substantial interventions we need to make, to change the lives of people in the urban underclass.

My view about systems for urban policy is that the UDAG program was a very good program, especially when we started. It created a competition where you had to put city officials and private investors together and get commitment and move the power structure. That's the kind of institution building that I think can and does make a difference. We need to recognize that we have to target, and focus on, the toughest urban problems if we want to

change the human environment and the nature of the urban communities.

We need to do things in a similar way in prisons. The prison population is mounting. Prison costs are going up rapidly. We need to invent ways to change our correction system so that we can rehabilitate young offenders with education, training, remediation, putting them on paths that will prevent recidivism. Many states are experimenting with new high-discipline, intensive service programs to do this.

One of my favorite philosophers is H. L. Mencken, Mencken said. "For every human problem in the world, there is a solution that is quick, simple, and wrong." We have, I hope, begun to identify some ways to move on this challenging urban agenda. Twenty years after the Kerner Commission Report, it is a different agenda - tougher, more stubborn, and more concentrated. This has to be part of the deliberations of people who care about remaking cities.

Winthrop's "City on the Hill" was the phrase he would later use in Boston. But it was in Pittsburgh that he signed on to the City's cause. And it was from Pittsburgh that the urban agenda of the New Frontier and the Great Society came, beginning with the appointment of Bob Weaver as the first black Cabinet member in American history, through the first housing subsidy programs, rent supplements, and Model Cities, to the watershed 1968 Housing and Urban Development Act, with its commitment to 26 million units of housing, six million for the poor, in the next 10 years.

All memories of John Kennedy are bittersweet: gratitude for having had his presidency, appreciation for what Lyndon Johnson did in his stead for civil rights and the city, everlasting regret that the bullets of Dallas kept him from finishing the course.

And somehow the mood of regret, frustration, sorrow, and anger is intensified when one looks at American urban regions today - the Entrepreneurial City and the Urban Village - and contrasts the present reality with the hopes and goals and strategies we first fashioned here 27 long years ago.

It is in the context of these memories of those aspirations that I want to treat three topics today: the political and civic significance of the new urban form of entrepreneurial cities and urban villages just taking shape across the country, their benefits and costs; what is missing in these new urban forms - in particular the crisis state of urban housing and urban schools. And finally, what might be done about what is missing? How might we repair the damage of a decade of neglect and show again a concern for the city's people, in the way John Kennedy expressed.

The new urban form: the entrepreneurial city and the urban village. In this remembrance of things past, it is incumbent on me to deal honestly and somewhat harshly with the new urban form in its two principal parts: the Entrepreneurial City and the Urban Village. Robert McNulty coined the term "American Entrepreneurial City" and Mayor William Hudnut, ill, of Indianapolis has popularized it. It depicts the "hot" urban places where national developers conclude complex equity-conditioned, lease-back, front-loading financial arrangement for commercial ventures which architects will enclose in shining glass towers. In Hudnut's terms, entrepreneurial cities "take risks," combat urban blight "not by going to Washington and asking for more but by creative use of non-traditional resources on the home front." They are "cost-conscious" but sophisticated, treating "cultural amenities as economic assets."

So post-modernist high-rises, new yuppy boutique retail centers, harborside bazaars, sport domes, and convention centers appear. They are sparked by the economic forces of the nation's transition from smokestack to service economy, the political forces of tax breaks, write-offs, and shelters of the 1981 Tax Act and the 1983 Banking Deregulation Act - incentives that have increased eight times since 1980 from \$2 billion to \$19 billion.

The sheer volume of commercial construction bears notice: 76 million square feet of new office space built in 22 major cities in 1984; the national rate of commercial and industrial space placed under construction exceeded one billion feet in 1986.

More than office space was involved in the 1980 urban renaissance of the Entrepreneurial City. As Bernard Frieden and Lynn Sagalyn show, the central city came alive once again as a market place. Beginning in the 1970's and continuing exuberantly through the mid-1980's, a golden age of downtown retail development emerged. Office district construction came first, but it was quickly followed by new hotels, convention centers, sports centers, restored waterfronts, and historic neighborhoods. Faneuil Hall in Boston and Chicago's Water Town Place became the prototypes for some 100 downtown shopping malls built in 70 cities from 1970 to 1986. Between 1973 and 1983 the number of downtown hotel rooms doubled in Atlanta, Boston, Philadelphia, St. Paul, Seattle, and Washington. In 1970 only 15 cities had convention centers that could handle a trade show for 20,000 people. By 1985, 150 cities had these facilities.

The second dominant feature of the contemporary American scene is a new form of suburban development. "Urban villages," according to Charles Lockwood and Christopher Leinberger and "outer cities" in William Stevens's terms suggest that "suburbs, once dependent on the central city, have metamorphosed into vast, glittering, independent 'outer cities' that rival and often surpass the traditional big-city downtown as centers of economic power and vitality." Both studies identify "cities"

like Princeton in New Jersey, Tysons Corner outside of Washington, Hunt Valley near Baltimore, Walnut Creek east of San Francisco, Post Oak-Galleria next to Houston as new "office, industrial, retail housing, and entertainment focal points amidst a low density cityscape." These new villages, with a new kind of downtown, exhibit both the "quality" and "quantity" of traditional centers. They are encouraged by such forces as the more attractive architectural features of modern commercial and industrial parks, the shift from rail to truck transport, recent telecommunication advances, cheaper land, and most of all, by the sheer difficulty of access to the large central cities.

So the urban villages capture a larger and larger percent of the new commercial construction around Atlanta, Los Angeles, New York. Constrained primarily (according to Lockwood and Leinberger) by the shortage of affordable, non-subsidized urban housing for the lower-middle-income worker and by growing urban traffic gridlock, these centers can overcome even these handicaps through high-density apartments and expanded highway and road construction. Given sensible government reorganization schemes, featuring metropolitan planning and zoning boards, the authors applaud the almost "irreversible" trend. "The opportunity," they say, "for all kinds of Americans to live, work, shop, play in the same geographical area - while retaining easy access to other urban village cores - seems almost too good to be true."

What's missing housing and schools. "Almost too good to be true" are Lockwood and Leinberger's terms indeed. What is missing from the euphoric accounts of the new urban form are three simple series of statistics: the growth in the number of urban immigrants, the growth in the number of urban poor, and the dramatic drop in the construction of housing that even middle-class families can afford.

The urban immigrant and the urban poor figures are familiar ones, though they are not widely shunted about. Annually almost 600,000 newcomers enter the U.S. legally and about the same illegally - increasingly from Latin America and Asia. More than half of them settle in cities. Cities also hold the lion's share of our 34 million poor people, native and immigrant, for 34 percent of black Americans remain below the poverty line.

What is less familiar are the statistics on housing, after the Reagan Administration savaged HUD program after HUD program - cutting the Department budget by 57 percent from \$36 billion to \$18 billion between 1980 and 1987; reducing authorization for assisted housing from \$27 billion to \$7.5 billion, the units of rental housing from 129,000 to 19,000 annually; reducing public housing reservations 93 percent from 205,000 to 14,000 units; and shifting to a voucher program in which 62 percent of the applicants reported no housing was available to them.

Given this meatcleaver of a housing policy, it is little wonder that three Rutgers colleagues, David Schwartz, Richard Ferlanto, and Daniel Hoffman, in a new book out this spring, cry crisis for American urban housing. They trace a decline in "starter" home ownership - first purchase by families in the 25 to 29-year-old bracket - between 1981 and 1987 from 42 percent to 36 percent and an increase in the average home price from \$52,000 to \$92,000. They estimate that 10 million Americans now live in overcrowded, unsound rental stock, that the shortfall for housing for the poor exceed one million units a year - the most reliable estimate for the numbers of homeless today. Overall, they find that 24 million Americans "have a housing problem," live in quarters substandard, overcrowded, or excessively costly. Indeed, compared to 1973, the 30-year-old wage earner earns less today but spends 40 percent of his or her income in mortgage payments compared to 21 percent then.

For the 1990's a conservative requirement for housing - either new or rehabilitated - is 8.4 million units. It breaks down like this: 1.7 million for "starter" homes for young families; 1.7 million for "old-elderly" (over 75); 3.4 million for the poor - an annual average of 680,000 units. Lest that seem Utopian, please recall that in the late 60's and 70's, the annual production rate exceeded 1.5 million units.

If housing for all but the well-to-do is an obvious challenge, so is the need for education - especially in the inner cities, especially in the new economy, especially in two age cohorts - the "baby boom echo" children and the adults from 24 to 55 whom the new economy requires we train again. The critical ingredient of the service-information economy, well established and documented now, is the quality of human resources: in particular, the ready availability of people with interpersonal, communication, and cognitive skills. National, state, and local economies are in short supply of these skills as the current mismatch between available jobs and qualified applicants makes plain. A locality's capacity to meet the new human resources requirement is the critical determinant of its future.

So education - both traditional and continuing - becomes the first urban priority, a prerequisite to assuring a labor force capable of responding to emerging job demands. The child cohort of the 1980's is not large enough to meet this demand in the 1990's, even if it were properly prepared. The retraining of the 24-to-55 age cohorts is essential, and this task includes instruction at the most basic level: simple literacy.
